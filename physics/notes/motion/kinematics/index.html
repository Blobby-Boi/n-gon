Kinematics

These equations give us the average velocity and acceleration. They do not tell us the exact value of velocity or acceleration, just an average.

$$v_{\mathrm{avg}} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \quad \quad a_{\mathrm{avg}} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} $$


If an object's acceleration is constant the equations of motion are also true. They use exact values, not averages.

derivation of equations of motion for constant acceleration

The first equation is the average acceleration equation rearranged with acceleration as a constant value.

$$a_{\mathrm{avg}} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$$ $$a = \frac{v - u}{\Delta t}$$ $$a \Delta t = v - u$$ $$\large \boxed{v = u + a \Delta t}$$

The second equations comes from how average velocity works when acceleration is constant.

$$v_{\mathrm{avg}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(v+u)$$ $$\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \tfrac{1}{2}(v+u)$$ $$ \boxed{ \Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v+u)\Delta t}$$

The next one also starts with the average velocity for constant motion equation form above. We can plug our previous equation into this to remove the final velocity.

$$v_{\mathrm{avg}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(u + a \Delta t+u)$$ $$v_{\mathrm{avg}} = \color{#00e}u + \tfrac{1}{2}a \Delta t$$

This can be combined with the equation for average velocity.

$$v_{\mathrm{avg}} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$$ $${\color{#00e}u + \tfrac{1}{2}a \Delta t}= \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$$ $$u\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}a \Delta t^2 = \Delta x$$ $$ \boxed{ \Delta x = u\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}a \Delta t^2 }$$

The rest of the equations of motion are derived by plugging equations into each other to remove a variable.

I followed the derivations here and here.

$$v = u+a \Delta t$$ $$\Delta x = u\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}a \Delta t^{2}$$ $$\Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v+u)\Delta t$$ $$v^{2} = u^{2}+2a \Delta x$$

\(\Delta x\) = displacement [m] vector

\(\Delta t\) = time period [s]

\(v\) = final velocity [m/s] vector

\(u\) = initial velocity [m/s] vector

\(a\) = acceleration [m/s²] (constant) vector

Question: What are some situations when acceleration is constant?
answer
  • objects in free fall (9.8 m/s²)
  • objects at rest (0 m/s²)
  • objects in moving at a constant speed (0 m/s²)

    Acceleration of Gravity

    On Earth everything is pulled down at 9.8 m/s². Generally the letter g is used for this number. The rate is the same for cars, birds, puppies, apples, balloons, and everything. Other effects like air friction, thrust, and buoyancy can change the perceived acceleration. Acceleration from gravity also changes based on the mass of the planet and your distance to the center of the planet.

    g = acceleration from gravity on Earth = 9.8 m/s²

    2-D Motion

    Solving for 2-Dimensional motion can reuse the methods from 1-Dimension. We just have to divide the problem up into x and y information. The x information is typically horizontal and the y information is typically vertical. As long as x and y are perpendicular to each other the events in each direction don't affect each other. The x and y axises are considered independent from each other.

    In this simulation you can see what a graph of position, velocity, and acceleration looks like for 2-D motion.

    It helps to organize our information in an x column and y column with time separate. The x and y columns are completely independent of each other, but time is shared.

    Δt
    Δx Δy
    u u
    v v
    a a

    If an object is in free fall we know the gravitational acceleration is purely in the vertical and there is zero horizontal acceleration.

    a = 0 a = -9.8 m/s²

    We can separate equations equal to zero into two parts.

    $$0 = \Delta t (8.7 + \tfrac{1}{2}(-9.8) \Delta t)$$
    $$0 = \Delta t $$
