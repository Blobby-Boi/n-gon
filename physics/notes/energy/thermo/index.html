Energy Loss and Gain in a System



As the ball in the simulation falls, energy transfers from gravitational to kinetic. As it rises, energy transfers from kinetic to gravitational.

Energy is conserved. This means that if we add up the total energy of a system it will always be the same value, unless some energy enters or leaves the system we are tracking.

Turn friction on and observe what happens.


The kinetic energy gradually leaves the ball as it changes into a type of energy called heat.

Heat is the random motion of particles. More heat leads to higher temperatures.

So the internal energy of a system is always conserved, unless energy enters or leaves our system.

The first law of thermodynamics says that the change in internal energy of a system is equal to heat flow into a system plus work done by the system.

$$\Delta U = Q - W$$

\( U \) = total internal energy of a system [J]
\( Q \) = Heat, thermal energy added(+) or removed(-) from system [J]
\( W \) = work done by a system [J]
Example: Compressing a volume of gas does 100 J of work, but at the same time the gas losses 200 J of heat to the surroundings. What is the change in the internal energy of the gas? Will the gas increase of decrease in temperature?
solution $$\Delta U = Q - W$$ $$\Delta U = -200 - 100$$ $$\Delta U = -300\,\mathrm{J}$$ $$\text{The gas will decrease in temperature}$$
Example: A 0.43 kg soccer ball kicked at 10 m/s rolls down a 30 m tall hill. We already solved this problem and got a final velocity of 26.2 m/s for the ball.

When actually performing the experiment we found at the bottom of the hill the ball only has a speed of 20.0m/s. Calculate the energy loss for the ball. How could the ball have lost energy?
solution

Find the difference between the experimental kinetic energy and the calculated kinetic energy.

$$\Delta KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^{2} - \tfrac{1}{2}mv^{2}$$ $$\Delta KE = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.43)(26.2)^{2} - \tfrac{1}{2}(0.43)(20.0)^{2}$$ $$\Delta KE = 147.6 - 86$$ $$\Delta KE = 61.6 \, \mathrm{J}$$ $$\text{The energy loss was probably from friction.}$$

Entropy

Entropy is hard to define. Several fields use the term to describe slightly different ideas. The different definitions of entropy are all loosely centered around the idea that entropy measures disorder. A high entropy system of particles is not organized. A low entropy system is very orderly. But how do you measure order?

Well, order is whatever you decide. Generally it is a state of a system that is able to do work. In the simulation below, the work we want done is rotating the rotor. What state of the system would make the rotor turn?

Click play and then click the simulation several times to add particles according to your definition of low entropy. If you chose well the rotor will turn on its own.

rotor torque

When the particles are evenly spread out the system is in high entropy. Adjust the slider to power the rotor. The spinning rotor lowers entropy and puts the system back in a state that can do work. Turn off the rotor and it will turn on it's own until it runs out of entropy.

Entropy can be defined in many other ways. Order defined as dispersal of temperature is useful for understanding engines. If a system has high entropy, the temperature is the same throughout the system. A low entropy system might have a sections with very low or very high temperature. These pockets of temperature difference can be made to do work, like in a combustion engine .

In chemical reactions, entropy is defined as low before an irreversible reaction or when chemical equilibrium is unbalanced. Some paper has low entropy before I burn it. After it's been burned it has high entropy.

Arrow of Time

The laws of physics are time reversible. Nature behaves the same moving forwards or backwards in time. The only exception is entropy. The second law of thermodynamics states that entropy of an isolated system doesn't decrease over time.

At the start of time entropy was very low. As time moves forward entropy increases. This means there is less entropy available to do work.

Stars convert low entropy hydrogen into higher entropy Helium and heat. As the heat from the sun strikes the Earth it lowers the Earth's entropy because it creates energy density differences. Those energy differences are harnessed by plants, and weather patterns, and solar panels to produce work.
